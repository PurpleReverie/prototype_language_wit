# Attention as a Moral Practice

By T Greig

_Draft — not for distribution._

## Abstract

This thesis argues that attention is not a passive cognitive state
but a moral practice — a sustained orientation of the self toward
what it does not yet understand. Drawing on Weil (1952, p. 117), Berger (1972, p. 9), and Arendt (1958, p. 79), I trace a triangulated account
of attention as labour, as ethical asymmetry, and as the
precondition for political action. The argument unfolds across
three substantive chapters; each closes with the same refrain:
the lamp burns only as long as someone tends it.

# Chapter 1: Introduction

## The problem

Attention, as Weil (1952, p. 117) argued, is the rarest form of
generosity — and the easiest to mistake for something else. We
say we are paying attention when we are merely looking; we say we
have considered when we have only consulted. Berger complicates
this Berger (1972, p. 9) by insisting that we never look at one thing
alone: the act of looking is always laden with the histories of
prior looking, with what the looker expects to find, with what
the institution funding the looker is willing to pay for.

What this thesis adds is the claim that attention, so understood,
is *labour* — and that the failure to recognize it as such has
moral consequence. To attend is to expend; the expenditure is
asymmetric; the asymmetry is political.

## The argument

I proceed in three steps. Chapter 2 develops the labour reading,
drawing on Arendt (1958, p. 79). Chapter 3 examines the asymmetry of
looking — who is looked at, by whom, on whose terms. Chapter 4
concludes by suggesting that the moral life is, in a precise
sense, an attentional life.

A note on method: the argument is *slow* on purpose.

# Chapter 2: Attention and Labour

## Arendt on labour

Arendt (1958, p. 79) distinguishes labour from work and action: labour
is what sustains the species, what must be done again the next
day, what leaves no monument. Most of human life, on this view,
is labour. The trouble is that attention has been written out of
this account — Arendt's *labour* is bodily, agricultural, the
keeping-alive of bodies. But the keeping-alive of meanings is
also labour, and it, too, must be done again the next day.

## Weil on the cost of looking

Weil's intuition is that the cost is moral as well as cognitive.
To attend, Weil (1952, p. 119) argues, is to suspend the self — to
hold the object as an *it* rather than as material for one's own
projects. Most of us cannot sustain this for more than a minute
at a time. The minute is precious.

## The asymmetric ledger

**Theorem 1** — Attention is asymmetrically distributed across the social field, and the distribution tracks power.

It does not follow that this is novel. It follows only that, if
the labour reading is correct, the politics of attention is not a
luxury topic — it is what most of our moral life consists in.

# Chapter 3: The Asymmetry of Looking

## The looked-at and the looker

Berger (1972, p. 9) is uncompromising on this: the act of looking is
not symmetrical. To be the object of a sustained gaze is to be
positioned, classified, available. Berger (1972, p. 47) extends the
point: in some institutional settings, the consent of the
looked-at is presupposed rather than asked for. Attention, here,
is not a gift but an extraction.

## Two cases

Consider the museum gallery and the surveillance camera. Both
require sustained attention; both produce records of what was
seen. They differ in who is permitted to look back. The museum
visitor may stare at the canvas for an hour and the canvas does
not stare back; the surveillance camera, by contrast, looks at
people who cannot, in the ordinary case, learn what was seen.

## What follows

If attention is labour and looking is asymmetric, then a serious
moral psychology of looking will start from this asymmetry rather
than from an abstract notion of the gaze. The next chapter draws
the threads together.

# Chapter 4: Conclusion

## What was argued

Three claims, in order. First: attention is labour, in something
close to Arendt (1958, p. 79)'s sense — it must be done again the next
day. Second: looking is asymmetric, after Berger (1972, p. 9). Third:
attending to something is a moral practice, after Weil (1952, p. 117), because it requires the suspension of the self.

## What this implies

It implies that the moral life is not chiefly a matter of choice
between actions; it is a matter of what one has paid attention to
long enough to be changed by. The agent who has not looked is
not, in any robust sense, a moral agent at all.
